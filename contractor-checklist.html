Contractor Checklist

Make sure you're hiring the right roofing contractor. Use this comprehensive checklist to verify and confirm critical items before signing any contract.

Protect Yourself

What to Verify Before Hiring a Roofing Contractor

Hiring the wrong contractor can lead to costly repairs, insurance issues, and safety hazards. Use this checklist to make sure your roofing contractor meets all the critical requirements for South Florida projects.

1

License Verification

✓ Verify the contractor holds a valid Florida roofing license (CCC or CGC prefix)
✓ Confirm the license is current and in good standing with the DBPR
✓ Verify the license holder is the one legally qualifying the company
✓ Check for any complaints or disciplinary actions on their license
2

Insurance Coverage

✓ Confirm the contractor carries general liability insurance
✓ Verify workers' compensation coverage for all employees
✓ Request a Certificate of Insurance (COI) directly from their insurer
✓ Make sure the insurance policy is active and won't expire during your project
3

Experience & Track Record

✓ Verify a minimum of 5 years of roofing experience in South Florida
✓ Ask for examples of completed projects similar to yours
✓ Check online reviews on Google, BBB, and Angi
✓ Confirm experience with your specific roof type (tile, metal, shingle, flat)
4

Installation Details

✓ Clarify if installers are W-2 employees or subcontractors
✓ If subcontractors are used, request proof of their licensing and insurance
✓ Confirm the scope includes full cleanup and debris removal
✓ Verify that rotted wood replacement and repairs are included
✓ Ask about stucco, drywall, and fascia repair as part of the job
5

References & Contract

✓ Request contact information for at least 3 recent customers
✓ Verify the written contract details every aspect of the installation
✓ Confirm the contractor's license number appears on the contract
✓ Review the payment schedule — never pay more than 30% upfront
✓ Ensure the contract includes start date, completion date, and scope of work
6

Permits & Warranties

✓ Confirm the contractor will pull all necessary building permits
✓ Verify the permit is in the contractor's name, not yours
✓ Ensure a final inspection by the building department is scheduled
✓ Ask about both manufacturer product warranty and workmanship warranty
✓ Get warranty details in writing — duration, what's covered, and exclusions

FAQ

Contractor Checklist FAQs

Common questions about hiring a roofing contractor in South Florida.

You can verify any Florida contractor's license by visiting myfloridalicense.com — the official website of the Florida Department of Business and Professional Regulation (DBPR). Search by the contractor's name or license number to confirm their license status, type, and any disciplinary history. A valid roofing license number starts with CCC (Certified Roofing Contractor) or CGC (Certified General Contractor).
At minimum, a roofing contractor should carry general liability insurance and workers' compensation insurance. General liability protects your property if the contractor causes damage during the project. Workers' compensation protects you from liability if a worker is injured on your property. Always ask for a Certificate of Insurance (COI) directly from their insurance provider — not just a copy from the contractor.
Florida law limits the amount a contractor can request as a deposit to a reasonable amount based on the project scope. Paying too much upfront puts you at risk if the contractor doesn't complete the work or goes out of business. A typical payment structure is 10-30% at contract signing, a progress payment at the midpoint, and the final payment upon completion and inspection. Reputable contractors like Roofing Palm never ask for excessive upfront payments.
The contractor should always pull the building permit in their name. This is critical because the permit holder is legally responsible for the work meeting code requirements. If a contractor asks you to pull the permit yourself, that is a major red flag — it may indicate they are unlicensed or trying to avoid accountability. The permit should also match the contractor's license number on file with the county.
A manufacturer warranty covers defects in the roofing materials (shingles, tiles, metal panels, etc.) and typically lasts 25-50 years depending on the product. A workmanship warranty covers the installation labor — if the roof leaks due to improper installation, the contractor is responsible for fixing it. Make sure your contractor provides both warranties in writing. At Roofing Palm, we offer up to 50-year product warranties and stand behind our workmanship.
Watch out for these warning signs: demanding full payment upfront, refusing to provide license or insurance documentation, door-to-door solicitation after storms (storm chasers), no physical business address, unusually low estimates compared to other bids, pressure to sign immediately, offering to waive your insurance deductible (which is illegal in Florida), and not willing to pull permits. Always get at least 3 written estimates and take time to verify credentials.
